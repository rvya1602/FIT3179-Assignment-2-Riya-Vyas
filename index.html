The State of Study: Education Trends in Australia

International Students by Country of Origin

In 2024, Australia continued to attract a significant number of international students enrolled in tertiary education, underscoring its strong reputation as a global education hub. The majority of these students come from Asia, with China leading at 189,107 students, followed closely by India with 138,373. Other Asian countries such as Nepal (65,529), the Philippines (39,223) and Vietnam (37,608) also contribute substantially, highlighting the region’s strong and growing presence in Australia’s tertiary sector. In addition, nations from other continents, such as Colombia (34,224), Brazil (23,946), and the United States (7,384), show that Australia attracts students from all around the world. This widespread international participation enriches Australian campuses both culturally and academically, contributing significantly to the higher education sector and the broader economy. Overall, the data reflects that Australia remains a highly sought-after destination for students.

Australian Population with Bachelor's Degree or Higher by State

In 2021, among Australians aged 15 years and above, there were notable differences across states in the proportion of the population holding a bachelor’s degree or higher. The Australian Capital Territory stood out with the highest percentage at 43.0%, reflecting its strong concentration of professionals and public service employees. Victoria (29.2%) and New South Wales (27.8%) also had relatively high proportions, indicating a well-educated population in the two largest states. Western Australia (23.8%), South Australia (22.7%), and Queensland (22.1%) had moderate levels, while Tasmania (21.9%) and the Northern Territory (21.5%) had the lowest percentages, suggesting smaller shares of degree holders in these regions. Overall, this data highlights the variation in educational attainment across Australian states and territories, with urban and administrative centers showing notably higher concentrations of tertiary-educated individuals.

All Students vs Commencing Students in Australian Universities (by State)

In 2024, the distribution of commencing and total students across Australian universities by state shows notable trends and variations compared with 2023. New South Wales hosts some of the largest student populations, with the University of New South Wales (82,272 total students) and the University of Sydney (78,825) leading in size, and both universities saw increases in commencing students compared with 2023, particularly UNSW, which grew by 28.7%. Victoria similarly has large institutions such as Monash University (93,159 students) and The University of Melbourne (75,513); Monash recorded a 19.1% rise in commencing students from 2023, while some smaller Victorian universities, like Federation University, experienced declines. Queensland universities show mixed trends compared with 2023, with The University of Queensland and Queensland University of Technology leading in size, but growth in commencing students ranges from small increases to slight decreases. Western Australia recorded strong growth in commencing students at some universities, including The University of Western Australia (+20.2%) and Curtin University (+15.9%) from 2023, though Murdoch University declined by 17.3%. South Australian universities mostly saw small declines or modest stability compared with 2023, while Tasmania’s University of Tasmania recorded a slight increase (+3.3%). The Northern Territory continues to have very small student populations, and the Australian Capital Territory saw declines in commencing students at both The Australian National University and University of Canberra compared with 2023. Overall, the data highlights that while Australia’s major universities continue to attract large numbers of students, the change from 2023 shows varied growth trends, reflecting differing regional dynamics and institutional appeal across the country in 2024.

International vs Domestic Student Numbers Over the Years

Between 2015 and 2019, Australia’s total tertiary student population grew steadily, reflecting the country’s strong appeal as an education destination. Domestic student numbers increased from 1,046,835 to 1,087,850, while international students grew even faster, from 363,298 to 521,948, highlighting the rising global demand for Australian higher education, particularly from countries such as China, India, Nepal, and Vietnam. The COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 and 2021 caused a sharp decline in international students, falling to 440,309, as border closures and travel restrictions limited mobility. Domestic student numbers, however, continued to rise, reaching a peak of 1,162,260 in 2021, showing the resilience of local enrolments despite global disruption. From 2022 onwards, international students returned in significant numbers, climbing to 589,288 by 2024, while domestic enrolments stabilized around 1,086,000. By 2024, the total tertiary student population reached 1,676,077, surpassing pre-pandemic levels. This trajectory illustrates not only a robust post-pandemic recovery but also the continued global attractiveness of Australia’s higher education sector, emphasizing the combined economic, cultural, and social contributions of both domestic and international students to the country.
